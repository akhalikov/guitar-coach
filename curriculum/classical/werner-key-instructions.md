# Werner Vol 1 — Key Instructions (from the book itself)

Foundational rules and advice extracted directly from Bradford Werner's *Classical Guitar Method Volume 1* (2020 edition), pp. 4–8. **These are non-negotiable — they're the author's stated pedagogical approach for Volume 1.** The full book is at `book/Classical-Guitar-Method-Vol1-Werner.pdf`.

---

## How to use the book (p.4)

> Study Part 1 in order, covering every piece on every page. Simultaneously begin Part 3 as a technique routine. Part 2 (chords) can be studied more loosely based on the skills and age of the student.

**Implication for the plan:** Part 1 is linear and complete. Part 3 (technique routines + scales) runs alongside Part 1 from the start. Part 2 (chords) is flexible — it can be interleaved or saved.

---

## Five Main Goals of Volume 1 (p.4)

These are the success criteria for "finishing" Vol 1:

1. Play solos and duets from start to finish with a steady tempo
2. Play legato melodies (also with open string bass accompaniment)
3. Play arpeggio pieces and patterns
4. Become proficient at reading music in first position (without key signatures)
5. Accompany basic songs with strumming or fingerstyle chords

---

## Right-hand technique policy: Free stroke only, no nails (p.5)

**This is the single most important thing to get right.** Werner is explicit:

> Teachers have different opinions about the use of rest and free strokes for beginners. I have seen good results from both approaches. My beginner students use free stroke until proper hand positions and a relaxed legato playing style are established. Students need not introduce right hand nails until hand positions and posture are secure. **I introduce rest strokes and nails during my Volume 2 method.**

**Rules for Volume 1:**
- **Free stroke only.** No rest stroke (apoyando). That's a Vol 2 technique.
- **No nails yet.** Keep nails trimmed short; play with the flesh of the fingertip.
- The goal is settled hand position and relaxed legato — not tone projection.

Once Vol 1 is complete and hand position is rock-solid, Werner introduces rest strokes and nails in Vol 2.

---

## Left-hand pinky rule (p.5)

> Students should use the left hand pinky for D and G (3rd fret of the 1st and 2nd strings). This fingering is required for solo pieces later and helps align the left hand. Students will have no trouble using the pinky if it is curved and in the proper position.

**Rule:**
- **String 1, fret 3 (D) → left-hand pinky (finger 4)**
- **String 2, fret 3 (G) → left-hand pinky (finger 4)**

Resist the temptation to use the ring finger (3). The pinky discipline pays off later in solo repertoire.

## Sitting & Hand Position Checklist (p.8)

**Sitting:**
- Head of the guitar at eye level (guitar at ~45° angle)
- Face of the guitar straight up and down (not angled back)
- Sit up straight, shoulders and neck relaxed
- Same position applies whether using a footstool or a guitar support

**Right arm & hand:**
- Right forearm rests on the guitar in front of the elbow
- Right wrist is straight with a relaxed arch
- Right hand plays around the rosette (not at the bridge, not over the fretboard)
- Right hand fingers move *into the palm*, not up and away
- Right hand thumb is *in front of* the fingers (not behind/under)

**Left arm & hand:**
- Left hand thumb is vertical and behind 2nd finger (no thumb-over-the-top)
- Left palm and knuckles parallel with the strings
- Left wrist is straight, not over-extended
- Left hand fingers are curved and contact the strings on the fingertips

## Tuning method (p.5)

Standard relative tuning sequence:
1. Play the 6th string at the 5th fret → tune open 5th string to that pitch
2. Play the 5th string at the 5th fret → tune open 4th string
3. Play the 4th string at the 5th fret → tune open 3rd string
4. Play the **3rd string at the 4th fret** (not 5th!) → tune open 2nd string
5. Play the 2nd string at the 5th fret → tune open 1st string

A clip-on tuner is recommended for setting reference pitch (low E = E2 = 82.4 Hz).

## Practice Advice (p.6) — Werner's nine principles

1. **Happiness in small goals.** Break difficulties into manageable pieces at a speed you can succeed at. Even a few notes played well is progress.
2. **Isolate difficulties and solidify strengths.** Work on hard material in the middle of the session; finish with something you play well so you end on success.
3. **Practicing is problem solving — not just playing.** Identify a problem, solve it, repeat. If your goal is 30 min of practice, that should be 30 min of focused work, not 30 min of noodling.
4. **Combine repetition with thoughtful reevaluation.** Some improvement comes from reexamining posture or hand position rather than just doing more reps. Don't bake in mistakes.
5. **No good days or bad days.** Every day is good for practice — but on hard days, slow the tempo down (use a metronome) until quality returns. You may play at half speed; that's fine.
6. **Play slowly.** Most students don't practice slowly enough. The majority of practice should be at very slow tempos. Only speed up once quality is locked in.
7. **Page markers.** Use sticky tabs on the pages you're working on so you can flip quickly between them.
8. **Keep it simple.** Even advanced players practice simple open-string exercises — at a very high quality level. Don't set unrealistic goals just to prove something.
9. **Trust your teacher.** If a teacher's advice differs from the book, follow the teacher.

## Music theory recommendation (p.5)

Werner recommends **Berklee Music Theory Book 1 — Intro to Theory** (with answer key) for students curious to dig into theory. He notes you don't *need* this for Vol 1, but it's a good companion if you want it.
