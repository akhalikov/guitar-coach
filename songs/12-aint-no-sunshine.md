## Why this song is special

- Written by Bill Withers in 1971 — one of the most famous blues hits of all time
- Key of **A minor** — same key group as Hurt
- Chords Am, Em, G — only three chords!
- The famous "I know, I know, I know..." moment — 26 repetitions 😄
- Ideal for vocal phrasing — Withers's voice moves right along the notes of the Em pentatonic
- Great material for working on groove and rhythmic feel

## What you're building

**Technique**
- Groove and rhythmic feel — the main skill for this song
- Arpeggio or soft strumming
- Dynamics — the song is very sensitive to pick attack
- Vocal phrasing on the Em pentatonic

**Theory**
- A minor — same key group as Hurt and Watching Over Me
- Am, Em, G — minimalism as an expressive device
- A 12-bar blues structure in a simplified form

**Music history**
- Bill Withers — one of the greatest soul/blues writers
- A direct link to the blues tradition: Robert Johnson → B.B. King → Bill Withers
- This song is in the Grammy Hall of Fame

## 🎸 Guitar and tone

### Which guitar to use?

**Squier Sonic Strat HSS** — position **5** (neck pickup) or **Tele SH** — neck pickup

You can also play it on a **classical guitar** — nylon strings give a warm, soft tone that fits the blues atmosphere of the song very organically. On classical, it sounds intimate and beautiful.

## 🎯 Focus when working on it

- Original tempo ~96 BPM — but it's not the tempo that matters, it's the **groove**
- Start very slowly — feel the rhythmic pattern
- Listen to how Withers sings "I know" — that's literally the pentatonic on guitar
- Pause between phrases works the same way as in Hurt
- The original uses fingerpicking on an acoustic — try it!

## 🎶 Improvising over this song

> 💡 **Which pentatonic fits?**
> The song is in **A minor**. Use **E minor pentatonic** — same position (open) as Hurt, Zombie, and Still Loving You. Withers's voice literally moves along these notes — find his melody on the fretboard.

| String | Frets |
|---|---|
| E (6th) | 0 — 3 |
| A (5th) | 0 — 2 |
| D (4th) | 0 — 2 |
| G (3rd) | 0 — 2 |
| B (2nd) | 0 — 3 |
| E (1st) | 0 — 3 |

> 🎤 **Vocal phrasing:** Sing "Ain't no sunshine when she's gone" and find that melody on the fretboard. Don't rush — Withers stretches every note with feeling.
